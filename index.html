Every 10 years, a nationwide census takes place to count every resident in the United States. This helps lawmakers understand the population growth (or decline) across the nation's 50 states.

With each decade's population change, the number of representatives granted to each state is adjusted in order to preserve the proportional nature of the U.S. House of Representatives. This process of reassigning the number of representatives is called reapportionment.

Along with adjusting the number of Congressional districts after reapportionment, each state must also redraw their districts so each district has roughly the same number of people.

The process of reapportionment and redrawing districts is called redistricting. So How do states decide how to redistrict their maps?

The U.S. Census breaks states into much smaller sections called voting precincts. Voting precincts are the building blocks that are used to make up congressional districts.

The map on the right shows the voting precincts in Maricopa County. By zooming in, you might be able to tell what cluster of precincts represent the city of Phoenix.

With thousands of precincts in each state, the number of maps that can be drawn are endless.

Fortunately, the U.S. Constitution and state constitutions include guidelines that must be followed, which reduces the number of valid maps that can be drawn.

Legally, districts must have equal population, be contiguous (or connected), and be geographically compact.1

To ease the complex redistricting process, mathematicians have created algorithms that redraw districts using objective math and simple logic.

The Shortest Splitline Method creates simply shaped districts. This algorithm splits the state's population in half using the shortest splitting line. The resulting hemi-states will be further split in half until the desired number of districts is created.

Created by Warren D. Smith
at The Center for Range Voting

Olson's algorithm creates optimally compact districts. It calculates how compact a district is by calculating the distance from the center to the furthest point in the district. The algorithm's goal is to have the smallest average distance to center over all districts in a state.

Created by Brian Olson at BDistricting

Like Olson's algorithm, the Voronoi algorithm seeks to create optimally compact districts. However, the Voronoi algorithm uses the sum of squared distances between residents and the center to measure compactness.

Created by Vincent Cohen-Addad, Philip N. Klein, and Neal E. Young at Brown University

While these algorithmically-created maps follow the aforementioned constitutional guidelines, they do not respect existing municipalities, cities, counties, or communities of interest- another guideline found in state constitutions.

Constitutional guidelines give us a strict framework to understand which maps are and aren't legally allowed. But for the thousands of maps that do follow all the rules, How can we tell which of maps are "better" than others?

Using Census, election, and geographic data, metrics about each map can be calculated. These metrics explain geographical and political features about a map, like how many counties are split or how many Democratic seats a map yields.

Compounding the values of one metric calculated from thousands of maps that follow constitutional guidelines, we can plot a histogram. With all the maps plotted, we can then compare where one specific map lies in relation to the rest, the average, and the median.

Researchers have been able to quickly create thousands of potential maps through the process of Markov Chain Monte Carlo. With a large and representative sample of a redrawn state map, we can compare one enacted state map to thousands using the metrics below.

Researchers under The ALARM Project at Harvard University provided an open-source Markov Chain Monte Carlo algorithm in R to create a representative sample of 5000 simulated maps for each state.

Expected Number of Democratic Seats

What are the expected number of democratic seats?
Using election data from previous years, we can simulate elections in redistricted states to calculate how many Democratic seats a map would yield.

In Pennsylvania, the average expected number of Democratic seats from all simulated plans is 8.1 seats out of 17. In contrast, the expected number of Democratic seats from the enacted plan is 9.3 seats out of 17. The enacted plan yields more Democratic seats than 100% of the simulated plans, showing that the enacted plan is a statistical outlier and gives the Democratic Party an advantage.

Election Results by Districts

What are election results by districts?
We can also use those simulated elections to compare the enacted plan to the simulated plans at the district level. Using the Democratic vote percentage, we can mark the most Republican to most Democratic district in each plan.

In Pennsylvannia, we note District 8 and 9, or the 8th and 9th most Republican district, in each plan. The majority of simulated plans have these districts yielding Republican seats, while the enacted plan's districts results in Democratic seats. This shows the enacted plan created two more majority-Democratic seats than the vast majority of simulated plans, giving the Democratic Party an advantage.

Packing found in Election Results by Districts

What is packing?
Packing is technique of redistricting that dilutes a political party's voting power by cramming voters of that party in as few districts as possible. This dilutes the party's voting power in all other districts.

In Alabama, the most Democratic district in the enacted plan, labeled District 7, has a Democratic vote margin more than 20 points above the average for the most democratic districts in all simulated plans. The higher margin in the enacted plan is caused by packing Democrats into one district, making that district significantly more Democratic and the remainder of districts significantly more Republican than their comparitive districts in all simulated plans.

Deviation from Partisan Symmetry

What is deviation from partisan symmetry?
Deviation from Partisan Symmetry can show how favorable a plan is to a political party. This metric measures the excess seat share above 50% that the Republican Party earns when their vote share is 50%.

In Ohio, the average deviation of all simulated plans is .03, meaning that the Republican Party yields 53% of the seat share with 50% of the vote. The enacted plan has a deviation of .11, guaranteeing the Republican Party 61% of the seat share. Because it yields more seat share with the same vote share, the enacted plan is more favorable to the Republican party than the majority of simulated plans.

Efficiency Gap

What is efficiency gap?
The Efficiency Gap compares the number of wasted votes between political parties. In districts where a party wins, any vote past 50% + 1 is a wasted vote. In districts where a party loses, any vote is a wasted vote. The efficiency gap is calculated by subtracting the number of wasted Republican votes from wasted Democratic votes. A positive efficiency gap indicates more Democratic votes are wasted.

In Florida, the average efficiency gap of all simulated plans is 0.06, while the efficiency gap of the enacted plan is 0.16. The enacted map has an efficiency gap greater than 100% of all simulated plans, showing that the enacted plan wastes Democratic votes at a much higher rate and gives an advantage to the Republican Party.

Average Polsby Popper

What is polsby popper?
Polsby Popper is a metric from 0 to 1 (a circle) used to measure the geographical compactness of a district. The average Polsby Popper of a map is found by averaging the Polsby Popper score of each district in a map.

In Illinois, the average Polsby Popper score for all simulated plans is 0.26. The enacted plan has an average Polsby Popper score of 0.14. The enacted plan's average Polsby Popper score is less than 100% of all simulated plans, meaning it is less compact than all the simulated plans and may include intentionally awkwardly-drawn districts for political advantage.

Counties Split

What are counties split?
Counties split counts the number of counties that are split amongst multiple districts.

In South Carolina, an average of 5 counties were split in all simulated plans, whereas 10 counties were split in the enacted plan. The enacted plan splits more counties than 100% of simulated plans, pointing to more intentionally drawn districts that do not follow the constitutional guideline (respecting existing counties) as best as it could.

With these partisan and geographic metrics, we are able to compare simulated and enacted maps. Comparing enacted maps with randomized simulations can help us understand if enacted maps are truly objective or have been heavily manipulated, but Exactly whose maps are we checking?

Written in Article 1 Section 4 of the U.S. Constitution, state legislatures have the responsibility to redistrict their states every ten years after the Census.2

Naturally, those in power have an incentive to stay in power. By giving state legislators the responsibility to draw the exact districts they are elected from, those in power are able to select the exact voters they want, usually matching their own political party. This has inevitably led to an unfair redistricting practice called gerrymandering.

Gerrymandering is the practice of redistricting with the intent to disadvantage opposing political parties or advantage one's own political party.

Thankfully, gerrymandering has become easier to uncover through the same process of how we analyzed metrics and maps above. This type of analysis was used by the Supreme Court in deciding two federal cases against the North Carolina and South Carolina state legislatures for unfair redistricting practices.

In order to combat gerrymandering, some state legislatures have outsourced their redistricting responsibility to Independent Redistricting Commissions (IRCs). IRCs are made up of individuals that currently do not hold office, thereby removing the incentive to redistrict to one's advantage.

After the 2010 U.S. Census, only Arizona, California, Idaho, and Washington used IRCs to redistrict. By 2020, Colorado, Michigan, Montana, and New York switched to IRCs over their state legislatures.3 More states are beginning to see the value in shifting to IRCs, but How can we tell if IRCs are actually less likely to gerrymander?

Using the metrics from above, we can compare how districting plans created by IRCs compare to districting plans created by state legislatures. Specifically, we are looking to see if IRCs are less likely to produce maps that are statistical outliers when compared to 5,000 simulated plans. In other words, whether or not IRCs produce maps that are similar to the large sample of unbiased, simulated maps.

The percentile of where an enacted plan falls amongst the 5,000 simulated plans will tell us if it is a statistical outlier. Percentiles near extremes of 0% or 100%, also understood as further from 50%, are statistical outliers.

Change the x and y axes to display a metric of your choice. The scatter plot will then show the percentiles of states that do not use an IRC and states that use an IRC for your chosen metrics.

Two pairs to explore are the partisan metrics (Partisan Symmetry and Efficiency Gap) and the geographic metrics (Polsby Popper and County Splits).

For Expected Democratic Seats, the average percentile difference from 50% for all the states with an IRC is 7.66%, whereas the average percentile difference for the states without an IRC (non-IRCs) is 11.38%. With a lower percentile difference, it seems IRCs are less likely to create plans that are outliers (further from 50%) in the number of Democratic seats they yield compared to 5,000 objectively simulated plans.

For Partisan Symmetry, the average percentile difference for IRCs is 16.48% and for non-IRCs is 9.03%. For the other partisan metric, Efficiency Gap, IRCs' average percentile difference is 10.03%, while non-IRCs' is 22.13%. It seems that non-IRCs are less likely to produce a statistical outlier for partisan symmetry, but IRCs are less likely to produce an outlier for efficiency gap. This gives us an inconclusive answer of whether IRCs are better at avoiding partisan gerrymandering.

For Polsby Popper, the average percentile difference for IRCs is 9.17% and for non-IRCs is 6.37%. For County Splits, the average percentile difference for IRCs is 38.7% and for non-IRCs is 41.9%. Similar to the partisan metrics, the geographic metrics are also split, where non-IRCs seem less likely to produce an outlier for Polsby Popper and IRCs seem less likely to produce an outlier for county splits.

There doesn't seem to be a clear answer from 2020 if IRCs are better than non-IRCs in preventing gerrymandering under the five metrics reviewed. Because of different sample sizes for IRC vs. non-IRC (8 vs. 40), state requirements for redistricting, and political/geographical variations between state to state, direct comparisons of percentiles could get foggy.

We can reduce the number of independent variables by directly comparing maps made by a legislature and by an IRC for the same state, with the same state requirements and political/geographical variations. The only differences now will be the entity that made the map and the year it was made for.

There is no well-preserved shp.file for Montana's 2010 redistricting map, which is needed for the ALARM Project to create representative samples off of. Thus, we will only be analyzing three out of the four states that moved from state legislatures to IRCs from 2010 to 2020.

Change the x axis to display a metric of your choice. The graph will then show the change in percentiles for the chosen metric when the states used legislatures to redistrict in 2010 and IRCs to redistrict in 2020.

For three of the metrics, Expected Democratic Seats, Deviation from Partisan Symmetry, and Polsby Popper, IRCs in 2020 created maps that were in percentiles closer to 50% than their non-IRC counterparts in 2010. Non-IRCs did better for the remaining two metrics, Efficiency Gap and County Splits.

This leaves us in the same position as after the first graph: we cannot make a definitive conclusion whether IRCs are less likely to produce statistical outliers.

IRCs are a relatively new invention in our democracy, spanning less than half a decade. Additionally, we only have maps from 2010 and 2020 in the form of file needed to perform MCMC, which leads to an extremely small sample size and makes it harder to perform quantitative analysis.

Despite this, we can be sure IRCs have brought plenty of good into the redististricting process.

IRCs have made more competitive districts than the national average. In Arizona, the average margin of victory was 28 points lower than the national average, alluding to the creation of less "safe" districts that are purposely drawn to protect a candidate or political party. California also saw more tossups across their districts than the national average.4

IRCs also promote greater transparency and voter participation into the redistricting process. Many IRCs post each proposed plan on social media and their websites; some go even further and reach out to the voters for their input.

As more states understand the benefit of IRCs and separating the redistricting process from state legislatures, there is a steady trend of more IRCs, as well as politician, advisory, and backup commissions, across the nation.5 With this approaching rise in the sample size of IRCs, there can soon be more definitive and quantitative research on IRCs.
