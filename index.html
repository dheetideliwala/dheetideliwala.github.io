Every 10 years, a nationwide census takes place to count every resident in the United States. This helps lawmakers understand the population growth (or decline) across the nation's 50 states.

With each decade's population change, the number of representatives granted to each state is adjusted in order to preserve the proportional nature of the U.S. House of Representatives. This process of reassigning the number of representatives is called reapportionment.

Along with adjusting the number of Congressional districts after reapportionment, each state must also redraw their districts so each district has roughly the same number of people.

The process of reapportionment and redrawing districts is called redistricting. So How do states decide how to redistrict their maps?

The U.S. Census breaks states into much smaller sections called voting precincts. Voting precincts are the building blocks that are used to make up congressional districts.

The map on the right shows the voting precincts in Maricopa County. By zooming in, you might be able to tell what cluster of precincts represent the city of Phoenix.

With thousands of precincts in each state, the number of maps that can be drawn are endless.

Fortunately, the U.S. Constitution and state constitutions include guidelines that must be followed, which reduces the number of valid maps that can be drawn.

Legally, districts must have equal population, be contiguous (or connected), and be geographically compact.

To ease the complex redistricting process, mathematicians have created algorithms that redraw districts using objective math and simple logic.

The Shortest Splitline Method creates simply shaped districts. This algorithm splits the state's population in half using the shortest splitting line. The resulting hemi-states will be further split in half until the desired number of districts is created.

Created by Warren D. Smith
at The Center for Range Voting

Olson's algorithm creates optimally compact districts. It calculates how compact a district is by calculating the distance from the center to the furthest point in the district. The algorithm's goal is to have the smallest average distance to center over all districts in a state.

Created by Brian Olson at BDistricting

Like Olson's algorithm, the Voronoi algorithm seeks to create optimally compact districts. However, the Voronoi algorithm uses the sum of squared distances between residents and the center to measure compactness.

Created by Vincent Cohen-Addad, Philip N. Klein, and Neal E. Young at Brown University

Maps can also be redrawn to give one political party a competitive advantage or to make districts maximally competitive, while still following all four constitutional guidelines.

But these political maps could land states in judicial battles about a person's right to vote and be represented fairly.

Constitutional guidelines give us a strict framework to understand which maps are and aren't legally allowed. But for the thousands of maps that do follow all the rules, How can we tell which of maps are "better" than others?

Using Census, election, and geographical data, metrics about each map can be calculated. These metrics explain geographical and political features about a map, like how many counties are split or how many Democratic seats a map yields.

Compounding the values of one metric calculated from thousands of maps that follow constitutional guidelines, we can plot a histogram. With all the maps plotted, we can then compare where one specific map lies in relation to the rest, the average, and the median.

Researchers have been able to quickly create thousands of potential maps through the process of Markov Chain Monte Carlo. With a large and representative sample of a redrawn state map, we can compare one enacted state map to thousands using the metrics below.

Expected Number of Democratic Seats

Using election data from previous years, we can simulate elections in redistricted states to calculate how many Democratic seats a map would yield.

In Pennsylvania, the average expected number of Democratic seats from all simulated plans is 8.1 seats out of 17. In contrast, the expected number of Democratic seats from the enacted plan is 9.3 seats out of 17. The enacted plan yields more Democratic seats than 100% of the simulated plans, showing that the enacted plan is a statistical outlier and gives the Democratic Party an advantage.

Election Results by Districts

We can also use those simulated elections to compare the enacted plan to the simulated plans at the district level. Using the Democratic vote percentage, we can mark where each district for each map falls.

Still following Pennsylvannia, we note Districts 8 and 9. Both of these districts have a majority of simulated plans resulting in Republican seats, while the enacted plan results in Democratic seats. This shows the enacted plan created two more majority-Democratic seats than the vast majority of simulated plans, giving the Democratic Party an advantage.

Packing found in Election Results by Districts

Packing is technique of redistricting that dilutes a political party's voting power by cramming voters of that party in as few districts as possible. This dilutes the party's voting power in all other districts.

In Alabama, District 7 in the enacted plan has a Democratic Party vote margin more than 20 points above the average for that district from all simulated plans. The difference in margin is created from packing Democrats into District 7, making that one district significantly more Democratic and the remainder of districts significantly more Republican.

Deviation from Partisan Symmetry

Deviation from Partisan Symmetry can show how favorable a plan is to a political party. This metric measures the excess seat share above 50% that the Republican Party earns when their vote share is 50%.

In Ohio, the average deviation of all simulated plans is .03, meaning that the Republican Party yields 53% of the seat share with 50% of the vote. The enacted plan has a deviation of .11, guaranteeing the Republican Party 61% of the seat share. Because it yields more seat share with the same vote share, the enacted plan is more favorable to the Republican party than the majority of simulated plans.

Efficiency Gap

The Efficiency Gap compares the number of wasted votes between the Republican and Democratic Party. In districts where a party wins, any vote past 50% + 1 is a wasted vote. In districts where a party loses, any vote is a wasted vote. The efficiency gap is calculated from subtracting the number of lost Democratic votes from lost Republican votes. A positive efficiency gap indicates more Democratic votes are wasted.

In Florida, the average efficiency gap of all simulated plans is 0.06, while the efficiency gap of the enacted plan is 0.16. The enacted map has an efficiency gap greater than 100% of all simulated plans, showing that the enacted plan wastes Democratic votes at a much higher rate and gives an advantage to the Republican Party.

Average Polsby Popper

Polsby Popper is a metric from 0 to 1 (a circle) used to measure the geographical compactness of a district. The average Polsby Popper of a map is found by averaging the Polsby Popper score of each district in a map.

In Illinois, the average Polsby Popper score for all simulated plans is 0.26. The enacted plan has an average Polsby Popper score of 0.14. The enacted plan's average Polsby Popper score is less than 100% of all simulated plans, meaning it is less compact than all the simulated plans and may include intentionally awkwardly-drawn districts for political advantage.

Counties Split

Counties split counts the number of counties that are split amongst multiple districts.

In South Carolina, an average of 5 counties were split in all simulated plans, whereas 10 counties were split in the enacted plan. The enacted plan splits more counties than 100% of simulated plans, pointing to more intentionally drawn districts that do not follow the constitutional guideline (respecting existing counties) as best as it could.

With these partisan and geographic metrics, we are able to compare simulated and enacted maps. Comparing enacted maps with randomized simulations can help us understand if enacted maps are truly objective or have been heavily manipulated, but Exactly whose maps are we checking?

Written in Article 1 Section 4 of the U.S. Constitution, state legislatures have the responsibility to redistrict their states every ten years after the Census.

Naturally, those in power have an incentive to stay in power. By giving state legislators the responsibility to draw the exact districts they are elected from, those in power are able to select the exact voters they want, usually matching their own political party. This has inevitably led to an unfair redistricting practice called gerrymandering.

Gerrymandering is the practice of redistricting with the intent to disadvantage opposing political parties or advantage one's own political party.

Thankfully, gerrymandering has become easier to uncover through the same process of how we analyzed metrics and maps above. This type of analysis was used by the Supreme Court in deciding two federal cases against the North Carolina and Alabama legislatures for unfair redistricting practices this past year.

In order to combat gerrymandering, some state legislatures have outsourced their redistricting responsibility to Independent Redistricting Commissions (IRCs). IRCs are made up of individuals that currently do not hold office, thereby removing the incentive to redistrict to one's advantage.

After the 2010 U.S. Census, only Arizona, California, Idaho, and Washington had IRCs to redistrict. By 2020, Colorado, Michigan, Montana, and New York changed to IRCs. More states are beginning to see the value in shifting to IRCs, but How can we tell if IRCs are less likely to gerrymander?
