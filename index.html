Every 10 years, a nationwide census takes place to count every resident in the United States. This helps lawmakers understand the population growth (or decline) across the nation's 50 states.

With each decade's population change, the number of representatives granted to each state is adjusted in order to preserve the proportional nature of the U.S. House of Representatives. This process of reassigning the number of representatives is called reapportionment.

Along with adjusting the number of Congressional districts after reapportionment, each state must also redraw their districts so each district has roughly the same number of people.

The process of reapportionment and redrawing districts is called redistricting. So How do states decide how to redistrict their maps?

The U.S. Census breaks states into much smaller sections called voting precincts. Voting precincts are the building blocks that are used to make up congressional districts.

The map on the right shows the voting precincts in Maricopa County. By zooming in, you might be able to tell what cluster of precincts represent the city of Phoenix.

With thousands of precincts in each state, the number of maps that can be drawn are endless.

Fortunately, the U.S. Constitution and state constitutions include guidelines that ought to be followed, which reduces the number of valid maps that can be drawn.

Legally, districts must have equal population, be contiguous (or connected), be geographically compact.

To easily sift through the complex nature of Census precincts and requirements, mathematicians have created a few algorithms that redraw districts using objective math and simple logic.

The Shortest Splitline Method seeks to create unbiased and simple-shaped districts. The Shortest Splitline Method splits the state's population in half using the shortest splitting line. The resulting hemi-states will be further split in half until the desired number of districts is created.

Olson's algorithm seeks to create optimally compact districts. It calculates how compact a district is by calculating the distance from the center to the furthest point in the district. The algorithm's goal is to have the smallest average distance to center over all districts in a state.

Like Olson's algorithm, the Voronoi algorithm seeks to create optimally compact districts. However, the Voronoi algorithm uses the sum of squared distances between residents and the center to measure compactness.

Maps can also be redrawn to give one political party a competitive advantage or to make districts maximally competitive, while still following all four constitutional guidelines.

But these political maps could, and historically have, landed states in judicial battles about a person's right to vote and be represented fairly.

Constitutional guidelines give us a strict framework to understand which maps are and aren't legally allowed. But for the thousands of maps that do follow all the rules, How can we tell which of maps are "better" than others?

Using Census, election, and geographical data, metrics about each map can be calculated. These metrics can explain geographical and political features about a map, like how many counties are split or how many Democratic seats a map yields.

Compounding the values of one metric calculated from thousands of maps that follow constitutional guidelines, we can plot a histogram. With all the maps plotted, we can then compare where one specific map lies in relation to the rest, the average, and the median.

Whose maps are we checking?

Written in Article 1 Section 4 of the U.S. Constitution, state legislatures have the responsibility to redistrict their states every ten years after the Census.


Naturally, those in power have an incentive to stay in power. So, giving state legislators the responsibility to draw districts, and ultimately choose their voters, in a way that advantages them in elections opens up the possibility of unfair redistricting practices.


The practice of redistricting with the intent to disadvantage the opposing political party or advantage one's own political party is called gerrymandering.


Thankfully, gerrymandering has become easier to uncover through the same process of how we analyzed metrics and maps above. That type of analysis was actually used in Supreme Court cases against the North Carolina and Alabama legislatures this past year.


In order to combat gerrymandering, some state legislatures have outsourced their redistricting responsibility to Independent Redistricting Commissions. These are made up of individuals who do not hold office, thus removing the incentive to redistrict to one's advantage.
